COMPLEXITY

 

Measuring Complexity

Charles Hallenbeck, Ph.D.

September, 2007

Visual inspection of a sound wave quickly reveals whether the sound is simple or complex. Listening to the sound is also a useful way to identify its simplicity or complexity. Those are subjective, intuitive methods. The viewer or listener is hard pressed to say exactly what it is that makes some sounds simple and others complex. What is needed is a way to quantify complexity, so that without viewing or listening to a sound, its complexity can be expressed in numerical terms.

Wedit introduces a measure of complexity of a sound (version 1.0.38 and later) that shows promise. It is based on a ratio between the number of inflection points in a sound wave, i.e. changes of direction, and the number of times the sound changes from a plus value to a minus value or vice versa, i.e., the zero crossings. The measure has a minimum value of 1.0, and ranges upward without limit as a sound becomes more complex. The complexity value is reported by Wedit whenever a sound wave is smoothed, and it is also included in the statistical display produced by the "list" command of the Wedit 3rd floor.

The simplest audio signal changes direction only once between successive zero crossings. Examples are the sine wave, the sawtooth or triangular wave, and the square wave. Such sounds have a complexity of 1.0 as reported by Wedit. The measure does not depend on the loudness or the frequency of the sound.

Between any two successive zero crossings, a sound wave must change directions an odd number of time. The Wedit measure counts only those changes of direction that promise to return to zero, i.e., only the odd numbered inflection points. Every time such an odd numbered inflection point occurs, a local maximum (or minimum) is created. Thus another interpretation of the complexity measure is a ratio between the number of local maxima or minima and the number of zero crossings.

Early experience with this measure suggest that smaller values are easier listening, with values over about 3.0 becoming progressively less satisfactory. A particularly noisy track might have a value over 10.0, and values of 100.0 and higher have been observed. When the time span of a track is displayed with a breakdown of tenths, the complexity value clearly identifies the location within the track of simpler and more complex portions. It is hoped that this measure will greatly aid the editing process, adding another analytical tool to those still valuable subjective ones.
